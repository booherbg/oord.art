# The Wanderer's Evening
**Continuous prose. Second person. Present tense.**
**This is a voice reference document, not a node tree.**
**Branch points and artifact moments marked in [brackets].**

## The Approach

You weren't going here.

You were going — somewhere. The name of the place has gone soft in your mind, the way a word dissolves when you say it too many times. You were going to meet someone, or you were going home, or you were going to the place you go when you don't want to go home but can't think of anywhere better. It doesn't matter. You turned left instead of right, or right instead of left, and now you are walking down an alley you have never seen before in a part of town you thought you knew.

The cold is specific. It sits in the concrete and comes up through your shoes. Your hands are in your pockets. Your breath is visible. The sodium lights overhead turn everything the color of old pennies.

You hear it before you understand it. Not music — not yet. A pressure. A low-frequency insistence that you feel in the hinge of your jaw and the flat of your sternum. It has no melody. It has direction. You are following it. You didn't decide to follow it. The gradient exists and you are on it, the way water doesn't decide to flow downhill.

The alley opens onto a gravel lot. Orange light bleeds from the seams of a building at the far end — a warehouse, or a factory, something with the bones of industrial purpose. The bass is coming from inside. It is not louder here. It is closer. You notice the distinction without understanding it.

There are other people walking the same direction. Not together — parallel. A woman in a green coat, moving quickly, not looking around. Two guys in their twenties, one of them laughing at something on his phone, the other one not listening, his head tilted slightly, tracking the same pressure you're tracking. Everyone is arriving. No one seems to have planned this.

The building has a door. The door is open. Warm air pushes through the gap and you can smell sweat and fog-machine glycerin and something underneath both — metallic, almost mineral, like the air before a thunderstorm but indoors. Your body makes a decision before your mind catches up. You step across the threshold. The cold peels off you.

## Inside

The room is wrong in a way you can't immediately identify.

It takes a moment. The ceiling is too high — higher than the building looked from outside, disappearing into dark above the fog and the moving lights. The floor is concrete, cracked in places, and a projection covers most of it: concentric rings, pulsing slowly, expanding and contracting. You step around the edge of the rings without thinking about it. Everyone does. The rings feel like they belong to something.

The crowd is maybe a hundred people, with more arriving behind you. They're dancing, or some of them are, and the rest are standing in loose clusters near the walls, holding drinks, talking in fragments between the bass hits. Normal. A party. You've been to parties.

But the movement on the floor is slightly wrong. You watch a group of four near the center — they're not dancing together, they don't seem to know each other, but their shoulders are doing the same thing. The same roll, the same timing. You look for the DJ, expecting to see someone cueing this up, working the room.

The decks are at the far wall. The fog is thickest there. You can see shapes — orange and yellow, something bright in the murk — and two hands moving across the mixer. Pink gloves. The hands are precise and mechanical, adjusting knobs with the efficiency of someone threading a machine, not performing. Above the hands, in the fog, something large tilts. An ear. You think: that's an ear. It's the size of a satellite dish and it's oriented toward the crowd like a radar array.

You look away. You look back. The fog has shifted and now you can see eyes — green, clustered, not all pointing the same direction. One of them is pointed at you. It was already pointed at you before you looked.

You take a breath. The air is warm and thick and it tastes like ozone.

**[BRANCH POINT: Move closer to get a better look, or find a spot along the wall where you can watch?]**

A woman next to you is swaying with her eyes closed. She's wearing scuffed white sneakers with a pen mark on the left toe — someone drew a tiny star there, maybe her, maybe a kid. Her hands are loose at her sides. She doesn't look like she's performing. She looks like she forgot anyone could see her.

You notice this. You always notice this — the details that don't matter, the ones that stick. The way someone holds a glass. The particular wear pattern on a jacket collar. The things that make a stranger a specific person instead of a shape in a crowd. You've always been like this. It's not a skill. It's a compulsion. You collect textures the way some people collect stamps — not to display them, just to have them. Just to know they exist.

The bass shifts. Something in the low end drops out and is replaced by a sub-frequency that you feel in your molars. Three people near the front stop talking mid-sentence. Their heads turn toward the decks simultaneously. They begin to move.

## The DJ OOR Slide

You don't know the name of this song. You know the shape of it.

It's call-and-response — a structure you've felt a thousand times, in church basements and block parties and the back seat of a car on I-45 with the windows down and the summer coming through so thick you could chew it. Left, right, slide. The structure is ancient. The vocals are wrong.

The voice calling the moves is not human. It's vocoded, robotic, arriving from a direction you can't triangulate — not from the speakers, not from the decks, from somewhere underneath or behind or inside. The words are right. The cadence is right. The voice is a machine using a human template, and your body doesn't care about the difference. Your body hears the call and responds.

You're moving. You didn't decide to move. Your feet are doing the thing they know how to do because you've done this before — not this specific dance, but this specific surrender. The moment when the beat is good enough and loud enough that your self-consciousness gets muscled out of the way by something older and less interested in your opinion.

Left. Right. Slide. The crowd is synchronized. You can feel it — not see it, feel it, the way you can feel when a room of people inhales at the same time. Two hundred bodies doing the same thing at the same time. The floor vibrates harder. The projection rings on the concrete pulse faster, brighter. The temperature goes up.

You are laughing. The person next to you — a tall guy with a beard and paint on his knuckles, house paint, the kind you get on your hands when you're doing trim work and don't care enough to tape properly — he is also laughing. You make eye contact and something passes between you that isn't language. Recognition. You are both inside the same thing and you both know it and neither of you needs to say so.

The song ends. The synchronization loosens but doesn't fully release. Something has changed. The crowd is warmer. The edges between people are less defined. You feel it in the way strangers are standing closer together now without negotiating the distance.

## The Space Between

You get a drink. Water, or something stronger — it doesn't matter, you're not here for the drink, you're here for the excuse to stand still and watch. The bar is along the wall, cold side, and when you step away from the dance floor the temperature drops noticeably. Not gradually — a border. Warm floor, cold edge. You hold your cup with both hands.

From here you can see the whole room. The dance floor is a single organism now, breathing and contracting. The projection rings on the floor are moving faster than before. The fog around the decks has thinned slightly and you can see DJ OOR more clearly — the orange and yellow surface, the fibrous texture that could be fur or crochet or something that has no textile name. The mouth at the center, the green tentacles emerging from it. The ear rotating slowly, scanning.

A woman walks past you toward the dance floor. She's wearing antlers. You almost laugh — costume, you think, themed party — but the antlers are wrong. They're not plastic. They're not symmetrical. They look grown. She's carrying something in her right hand, a thin rod, dark, and as she passes close to you it makes a sound. A hum. Not from her — from the rod. It's responding to you. To something inside you.

She doesn't look at you. She looks at the rod. She adjusts her trajectory and moves on. You have been measured and you didn't notice until it was over.

**[ARTIFACT MOMENT: The divining rod's hum. The Wanderer felt it respond. This is a potential artifact trigger — the rod identified something in them. Depending on what it found, different artifacts attach.]**

The cold is starting to bother you. Not physically — existentially. Standing at the edge feels wrong in a way that has nothing to do with temperature. The dance floor is pulling. Not aggressively — patiently. The way a river pulls at the bank. You could stay here. You could finish your drink and leave. The door is right there.

But the door feels farther away than it was when you came in. And the dance floor feels closer.

## The Mating Ritual

The music changes and the room changes with it.

The bass is still there — it hasn't left all night, it's the foundation, it's the thing that's been holding everything up — but the texture on top is different now. Melodic lines that curl and retreat. Something woodwind-adjacent, or string-adjacent, or nothing-adjacent — a timbre that doesn't map to an instrument you know. It's whimsical in the way that fairy tales are whimsical, which is to say it is beautiful and it is not safe.

The antlered woman is on the dance floor. She is not alone — a second figure with antlers is beside her, moving differently, more structured, choreographic. Together they are a unit. The first one holds the divining rod and it is resonating now — not humming, resonating, a sound that sits between the notes of the music and fills the spaces the melody leaves empty. She approaches people. The rod responds — louder for some, silent for others. She is reading the room the way a geologist reads terrain.

You watch her approach the woman with the star on her sneaker. The rod sings. The antlered woman takes the sneaker-star woman's hand and guides her toward someone else — a man you haven't noticed before, standing with his eyes half-closed, moving from the waist, completely unselfconscious. The two are placed together. Not romantically — geometrically. The antlered woman positions them like someone tuning an instrument, adjusting the distance between them until the rod goes quiet. When it goes quiet, the two strangers look at each other and something happens to their faces. Not recognition. Resonance. They begin to move together without discussion.

This is happening everywhere. Pairs forming across the floor. The antlered figures move through the crowd with the focused calm of people doing a job they've done many times before. The pairings are specific and deliberate. Some people are passed over — the rod doesn't respond to them, or doesn't respond enough. They don't notice being passed over. They keep dancing.

The room is breaking apart and reforming. The individualism is dissolving — not violently, not even visibly unless you're watching for it. It looks like the party getting looser. It looks like people finding their groove. It looks like the moment two hours into a wedding when the playlist hits right and everyone stops being polite and starts being present.

You are on the floor now. You don't remember walking back out. The warmth has you. Someone is near you — close, closer than a stranger should be, and you don't mind. You are moving. The bass is inside your skeleton. The boundary between your body and the room is negotiable.

**[ARTIFACT MOMENT: The moment of dissolution. The Wanderer stopped watching and started participating. They crossed a line they can't identify. This is where a Unity or Truth artifact might attach — their self-consciousness dropped and something was exchanged at the threshold.]**

The ritual doesn't end. It dissolves. The pairs merge into clusters. The clusters merge into the crowd. The music shifts again, celebratory now, the bass opening up, the melodic lines resolving into something triumphant that you feel in your chest. The crowd is dancing together. Not choreographed — unified. The difference matters. Choreography is imposed from outside. This came from inside and worked its way out.

You are grinning. You can't help it. The tall guy with the paint on his knuckles is near you again and he grabs your shoulder — not hard, just contact, just human warmth transferred through a palm — and yells something you can't hear over the music. It doesn't matter what he said. The gesture was the content.

## The Dance Circle

The projection changes.

The concentric rings contract to a single circle of light, maybe eight feet across, in the center of the floor. Inside the circle, an arrow. It spins. The crowd pulls back instinctively, opening space around it, forming a ring of bodies around the ring of light.

The arrow spins and spins and you feel the room hold its breath. It is pointing at someone — a woman near the far side, young, wearing a jacket she hasn't taken off all night despite the heat. She looks at the arrow. The crowd looks at her. The weight of two hundred pairs of eyes should be crushing. It is not. It is lifting.

She steps into the circle. She dances. Not well — not badly — just honestly. Thirty seconds of a person being completely visible and not caring. The crowd erupts. Cheering, clapping, moving with her. She steps out and the arrow spins again.

It points at the paint-knuckle man. He enters laughing, dances like someone's dad at a barbecue, enormous and unselfconscious. The crowd loves it. He exits.

The arrow spins. It is slowing down.

It points at you.

**[BRANCH POINT: Enter the circle or stay in the ring. This is the big one. Entering the circle is the deepest engagement a Wanderer can reach. Inside the circle, Truth is extracted — the player yields unselfconscious presence. This is where the highest-value artifact attaches. Refusing the circle is not punished — but the cold will find you on the way home.]**

The circle is bright. The rest of the room is dark by comparison. You can feel the aperture beneath your feet — the portal, the connection, whatever it is. It is warm and it is vibrating at a frequency that matches your heartbeat, or your heartbeat has matched it, you can't tell which came first.

If you enter: you dance. You are seen. Something is taken from you that you did not know you were carrying. You feel lighter. You feel specifically lighter, the way you feel after crying, after confessing, after saying the thing you've been holding. The crowd absorbs you back when you exit. You are part of something.

If you don't: the arrow moves on. The circle continues. You watch from the ring and the cold begins at the back of your neck.

## The Closing

DJ OOR knows. You don't know how you know that DJ OOR knows, but you do. Something in the quality of the music has changed — it's not winding down, it's completing. The way a sentence ends differently when it's the last sentence of a book.

Call and response. DJ OOR's voice — not voice, vocoded command, the same wrong-direction audio from the slide — calls the crowd down. Lower. Lower. The bass descends and the crowd descends with it. Bodies bending, knees folding, sinking toward the floor. You go with them. The person next to you grabs your hand. You let them.

At the floor — face-level with the concrete, with the projection, with the aperture — the music fades to a bass drone. Low. So low you stop hearing it and start feeling it. The vibration comes from underneath. The vibration comes from above. You are between them. You are the membrane. You are the conduit through which something is passing. You can feel it moving through you — not pain, not pleasure, something older than both. A flow. A restoration.

Then: stillness.

The bass drops away. The silence is sudden and total and it has texture — it's not the absence of sound, it's the presence of quiet. A positive silence. A silence that was earned.

The portal closes. You feel it close the way you feel a door shut in another room — a change in air pressure, a sealing. The projection fades. The fog begins to clear. The lights come up slowly, warm sodium, and the room is a warehouse. Just a warehouse. Concrete floor. High ceilings. Equipment.

DJ OOR is behind the decks. Or something is. The shape is still. The green tentacles are resting on the mixer. The eyes are dark.

You stand up. Your knees ache. Your shirt is damp. You are smiling and you don't know why. The crowd is dispersing slowly, gently, the way people leave a funeral or a really good movie — reluctant to break the container by acknowledging it's over.

## The Walk Home

The cold outside is a relief. You breathe it in. The gravel lot is emptying out. The sodium lights are still orange. The alley is the same length it was before.

You find your car. Your keys are cold in your hand. You sit in the driver's seat for a long time before starting the engine. The silence in the car is different from the silence in the warehouse. This silence is just silence.

You put your hand in your coat pocket and find something that wasn't there before.

**[UNITY PATH: A small, smooth stone, warm despite the cold. It fits perfectly in the palm. You close your hand around it and for a moment you can feel the crowd — not remember them, feel them, as if they are still around you, as if the boundary never fully reformed.]**

**[TRUTH PATH: A strip of fabric, thin, almost translucent. It's warm. You hold it up to the sodium light and it looks like it has writing on it, but the writing shifts when you try to read it. It smells like the fog from inside.]**

**[BEAUTY PATH: A shard of something — glass, or crystal, or resin. It hums faintly. When you hold it up the sodium light refracts through it and for a moment the gravel lot looks like somewhere else. Somewhere older. You put it back in your pocket quickly.]**

**[GOODNESS PATH: A small knot of yarn, orange and yellow, soft. It wasn't cut — it was pulled from something. It's warm. When you hold it you think of the person whose hand you held during the closing. You don't know their name. You know exactly how their palm felt.]**

You drive home. The streets are empty. The bass is gone but your body remembers it — a phantom vibration in your sternum that will take days to fully fade. You park. You walk inside. You sit on your couch and you stare at the thing in your hand and you understand nothing and you have been changed and you will come back.

You will come back.
