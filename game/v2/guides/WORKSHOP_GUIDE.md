# Prose Workshop Guide
**For: Blaine + Claude collaborative generative sessions**
**Purpose: The beat loop that produces good prose. Follow it.**

---

## The Core Loop

This is a two-person process. Neither side can do it alone. Skipping steps produces generic prose.

### Beat 1 — Blaine sets the scene physically
Tell me what the character is **actually doing** — hands, tools, posture, environment. Not what the scene means. What it looks like. "They'd be dialing in physical sliders, adjusting curves on the custom iPad interface" is the kind of direction that unlocks everything.

### Beat 2 — Claude drafts from character inward
Write from the **person** outward, not from the **thesis** down. Start with hands, eyes, posture. Let meaning emerge from observation. Never write toward a landing line.

### Beat 3 — Blaine redirects by vibe and behavior
Notes should be emotional targets expressed as feelings, not concepts. "Oh this guy seems alright — maybe this is fine, it's just magical and ephemeral" is a perfect note. "The scene should convey the non-contradiction of beauty and horror" is not.

Relationship and dynamic reframes are the highest-leverage notes. "They're collaborating professionally" produces completely different prose than "one is manipulating people."

### Beat 4 — Claude redrafts with new behavioral and emotional target
One pass, fresh. Don't patch the old draft. Write new.

### Beat 5 — Ship or iterate
If it lands, ship. If not, back to Beat 3 — Blaine gives another behavioral/vibe redirect.

## Why This Works

Draft 1 of w_005b (THE HANDS) was written thesis-outward: "beauty and horror are not in contradiction" → decorate with imagery. It felt like an essay wearing a scene's clothes.

Draft 2 was written character-inward: "what does this guy look like when he's working" → meaning emerged from the observation. Nailed it in one pass.

The difference: Blaine's direction was physical ("iPad, sliders, half-smile, frost on his jeans"), the thesis was killed ("the contradiction isn't the point"), and the emotional target was a vibe ("maybe this is fine").

## Rules for Claude During Generative Sessions

1. **Ask for the physical before drafting.** If Blaine hasn't described what the character is doing with their hands, ask.
2. **Never write toward a thesis line.** If you notice yourself constructing a scene to serve a final sentence, cut the sentence and let the scene breathe.
3. **Draft fresh, not patched.** When direction changes, rewrite from scratch. Patching a thesis-outward draft with character-inward notes produces Frankenstein prose.
4. **Concrete > abstract, always.** "iPad mounted to the rack, swiping through curves" beats "hands working with practiced efficiency."
5. **One line of dialogue can do more than a paragraph of description.** "Wild, right? Best low end I've ever gotten in a concrete room." — that line IS the character.
6. **The paranormal beat grounds the scene.** One impossible thing, observed together, reacted to differently. The human response to the impossible tells you who the character is.

---

## Rules for Blaine During Generative Sessions

1. **Lead with behavior, not theme.** The more physical and specific your direction, the better the draft.
2. **Give the vibe as a feeling.** "He seems like he's having the best night of his life" is a north star. "The scene should establish the Builder's relationship to craft" is a fog bank.
3. **Kill the landing.** If a draft is building toward a thesis sentence, say so. The scene is better without it.
4. **Reframe dynamics, not details.** "They're working together professionally" is a higher-leverage note than "change the wrench to a slider."
